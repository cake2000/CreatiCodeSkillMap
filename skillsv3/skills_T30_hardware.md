# T30 – Devices & Hardware: K–8 Skill List (Draft v1)

Topic reference: `T30 Devices & Hardware` in `domains_topics_overview.md`
Domain: Systems & Security (D4) · CSTA focus: SAS‑HW (Systems and Security – Hardware)

Each skill below has:

- a stable **ID** (`T30.G<grade>.<nn>`),
- an IXL‑style **short name**,
- a **description** (what understanding/behavior it targets),
- a **challenge format** (the typical problem type to assess it).

Where relevant, a primary **CSTA code** is noted.

---

## Grade K (PreK–K)

Grade K introduces the basic idea that computers and devices exist, and that they do things we ask them to do. The focus is on recognizing everyday tools and their purposes.

### T30.GK.01 – Identify basic computer parts (pictures)

- **Short name:** Name computer parts
- **Description:** Students recognize and name simple hardware components (display/screen, keyboard, mouse, system box/CPU unit) in pictures or on a real classroom computer. They understand that a computer is made of parts that work together.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, picture labeling. Show a labeled or unlabeled picture of a desktop computer or laptop. Students click/drag labels to identify the display, keyboard, mouse, and main unit. Auto‑grading checks that each part is correctly identified.
- **CSTA:** EK‑SAS‑HW‑01 (Examine tools to accomplish tasks).

### T30.GK.02 – Tell what a device does

- **Short name:** Explain what a device is for
- **Description:** Students identify what a common device or tool (tablet, smart speaker, classroom smart board, classroom light, door with motion sensor) is used for and name the action it performs (e.g., "lights turn on when someone walks in").
- **Challenge format:** Concept, multiple choice or sentence completion. Show a picture of a device or scenario and ask "What does this do?" or "Why would we use this?" Auto‑grading checks the selected or typed answer against acceptable descriptions.
- **CSTA:** EK‑SAS‑HW‑01.

### T30.GK.03 – Understand that programs make devices work

- **Short name:** Programs tell devices what to do
- **Description:** Students are introduced to the idea that computers and robots follow instructions written by people (programs) to perform actions. They recognize that a device's behavior comes from instructions, not magic.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, story or animation. Show a short video or story: a robot follows a program and performs an action (move, beep, light up). Students answer "Who/what told the robot to do that?" or choose from pictures ("instructions from a person," "magic," "the program"). Auto‑grading checks understanding of the program–behavior link.
- **CSTA:** EK‑SAS‑HW‑01.

## Grade 1

Grade 1 deepens recognition of hardware components and introduces the idea that hardware and software work together. Students can name basic components and understand that programs control behavior.

### T30.G1.01 – Describe purposes of computer hardware

- **Short name:** What does each computer part do?
- **Description:** Students explain the purpose of common hardware components: display (shows information), keyboard (user input), mouse (user input), system unit (processes/thinks), and speakers (outputs sound). They use accurate terminology.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, multiple choice or matching. Provide a list of components and a list of purposes (or vice versa). Students match each part to what it does. Auto‑grading checks all matches are correct.
- **CSTA:** E1‑SAS‑HW‑01 (Describe purposes of basic hardware components).

### T30.G1.02 – Recognize input and output devices

- **Short name:** Input vs output
- **Description:** Students distinguish between devices or parts that let you control a computer (input: keyboard, mouse, microphone, touchscreen) and devices that show or tell you information (output: display, speakers, printer).
- **Challenge format:** Concept, sorting or multiple choice. Show pictures or names of devices/components. Students sort them into input or output categories, or answer "Is a speaker an input or output device?" Auto‑grading checks categorization.
- **CSTA:** E1‑SAS‑HW‑01.

### T30.G1.03 – Understand hardware and software roles

- **Short name:** Hardware vs software
- **Description:** Students distinguish between hardware (the physical parts you can touch: computer box, keyboard, mouse, robot body) and software (the programs/instructions that tell them what to do).
- **Challenge format:** Concept, sorting or multiple choice. Show pictures and terms: "keyboard," "operating system," "robot arm," "video game," "mouse," "app," etc. Students sort into hardware and software categories. Auto‑grading checks all placements.
- **CSTA:** E1‑SAS‑HW‑01.

### T30.G1.04 – Recognize that programs run on devices

- **Short name:** Programs run on computers
- **Description:** Students understand that programs (apps, games, scripts) run on devices (computers, tablets, robots) and that the device executes the program's instructions to produce the behavior we see or interact with.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, matching or scenario. Show a device and a program (e.g., a tablet and a game icon), or describe a scenario ("I see a game moving on a tablet"). Students match programs to devices or explain how the program makes the device behave a certain way. Auto‑grading checks understanding.
- **CSTA:** E1‑SAS‑HW‑01.

## Grade 2

Grade 2 focuses on understanding how hardware and software interact. Students explore how data flows through a system and how sensors/devices respond to stimuli.

### T30.G2.01 – Justify software for tasks

- **Short name:** Choose the right software for the job
- **Description:** Students select and justify an appropriate software tool or app for a given task (e.g., "I would use a word processor to write a story," "I would use a drawing app to paint a picture"). They explain why that software is suitable.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, multiple choice with explanation or matching. Present a task and several app/software options. Students choose the best one and optionally explain why. Auto‑grading checks the selection and validates brief text explanations against common justifications.
- **CSTA:** E2‑SAS‑HW‑01 (Justify the use of software).

### T30.G2.02 – Understand input → processing → output

- **Short name:** How computers receive and send information
- **Description:** Students learn the basic I/O model: computers receive input (from keyboard, mouse, sensors), process it internally, and produce output (display, sound, movement). They trace simple flows such as "user types letter" → "computer shows letter on screen."
- **Challenge format:** Concept, sequence or flowchart. Show a simple diagram or scenario; students complete a flow like "Input: ___ → Computer: ___ → Output: ___" or order steps (first the person types, then the computer shows the letter, etc.). Auto‑grading checks the sequence or fills.
- **CSTA:** E2‑SAS‑HW‑01.

### T30.G2.03 – Recognize everyday sensors and their purposes

- **Short name:** Sensors in the real world
- **Description:** Students identify simple sensors in their environment (motion sensor doors, light sensors controlling classroom lights, thermostats, touchscreens) and describe what each sensor detects and how a device responds.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, matching or short answer. Show pictures of sensors and scenarios (e.g., a motion sensor, a classroom door opening). Students match sensor to what it detects or describe what happens. Auto‑grading checks matches or validates text answers.
- **CSTA:** E2‑SAS‑HW‑01.

### T30.G2.04 – Connect hardware and software in CreatiCode

- **Short name:** Software controls hardware in CreatiCode
- **Description:** Students create or trace a simple CreatiCode project where a program (code) makes hardware (a sprite, simulated robot, or connected device) respond. They understand the code→hardware link.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, starter project. Provided: a CreatiCode project with a sprite and a few blocks (e.g., "move," "turn," "say"). Students complete the program to make the sprite respond to a green flag click or simple event. Auto‑grading checks that the sprite executes the intended actions when the program runs.
- **CSTA:** E2‑SAS‑HW‑01.

## Grade 3

Grade 3 deepens understanding of how hardware components and software coordinate, and students explore simple troubleshooting and how systems handle input/output together.

### T30.G3.01 – Explain hardware–software interaction for I/O

- **Short name:** How hardware and software work together
- **Description:** Students explain that hardware provides physical components for input (keyboard receives a keystroke) and output (display shows pixels), while software interprets the input and decides what output to produce. The two work as a system.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, explanation with diagram or text. Present a scenario (e.g., "You click a button on the screen; a sound plays"). Students explain which part is hardware (the button, the speaker), which is software (the program), and how they work together. Auto‑grading checks for mention of both hardware and software and their roles.
- **CSTA:** E3‑SAS‑HW‑01 (Explain how hardware and software work together for I/O).

### T30.G3.02 – Identify sensors and predict device responses

- **Short name:** Sensors detect and devices react
- **Description:** Students identify sensors in devices they use (camera, microphone, accelerometer, touch sensor, temperature sensor) and predict how the device responds when the sensor detects something (e.g., a camera detects motion → video app records, a touch sensor detects a finger → light turns on).
- **Challenge format:** Concept, scenario matching or prediction. Present scenarios like "A motion sensor on a door detects someone" or "A microphone detects loud sound." Students predict or match the device's response. Auto‑grading checks the prediction or match.
- **CSTA:** E3‑SAS‑HW‑01.

### T30.G3.03 – Troubleshoot a simple hardware/software problem

- **Short name:** Fix a broken device
- **Description:** Students apply basic troubleshooting steps (checking connections, restarting, trying again) to solve common hardware and software issues (device won't turn on, app crashes, speaker has no sound). They explain what they tried and why.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, scenario-based troubleshooting. Present a problem (e.g., "A game on a tablet keeps stopping. What would you do?"). Students choose or describe troubleshooting steps from a list or text box. Auto‑grading validates choices against common solutions (check battery, restart app, free up memory, etc.).
- **CSTA:** E3‑SAS‑HW‑01 (implied through understanding system behavior).

### T30.G3.04 – Build a CreatiCode sensor simulation

- **Short name:** Simulate a sensor in code
- **Description:** Students create a CreatiCode project where they simulate a sensor (e.g., a "motion sensor" sprite that detects when another sprite is nearby, or a "button" that triggers an action). They use conditionals to check the sensor state and respond.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, starter project. Provided: two sprites (a sensor sprite and a target sprite). Students write code to detect when the target is near (using distance/touching checks) and trigger a response (change color, move, say message). Auto‑grading checks that the sensor logic works correctly across test positions.
- **CSTA:** E3‑SAS‑HW‑01.

## Grade 4

Grade 4 expands knowledge of sensors, embedded systems, and the interplay of hardware, software, and data. Students explore how real-world devices use sensors and algorithms.

### T30.G4.01 – Describe real-world sensor applications

- **Short name:** How sensors work in devices
- **Description:** Students describe real examples of sensors embedded in devices they encounter: motion sensor lights, smartphone cameras detecting faces, fitness trackers counting steps, thermostats regulating temperature. They explain what each sensor detects and how the device uses that data.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, matching or short-answer explanation. Show pictures of devices (motion light, smartphone, fitness tracker) and descriptions of how they work. Students match each device to its sensor behavior or explain what sensor enables a feature. Auto‑grading checks matches or validates text against key sensor concepts.
- **CSTA:** E4‑SAS‑HW‑01 (Describe the use of sensors).

### T30.G4.02 – Design a sensor-based system in CreatiCode

- **Short name:** Build a sensor-triggered project
- **Description:** Students design a CreatiCode project that simulates a sensor-based system (e.g., a light-activated room, a robot that avoids obstacles, a game where touching a sprite triggers a reward). They use sensor inputs (simulated via conditionals or collision detection) to control outputs.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, creative/starter project. Prompt: "Create a system where [sensor type] detects [event] and the [device] [responds]" (e.g., "Create a light system where a motion sensor detects a person and the light turns on"). Students build sprites and logic to simulate the sensor and response. Auto‑grading checks that the sensor is simulated correctly and the response is triggered on-demand.
- **CSTA:** E4‑SAS‑HW‑01.

### T30.G4.03 – Understand data flow from sensors

- **Short name:** Sensors send data to programs
- **Description:** Students understand that sensors generate data (measurements, yes/no readings, positions) that programs read and use to make decisions. They trace a data flow: sensor captures → software reads → software decides action.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, flow diagram or matching. Show a scenario and the steps: sensor detects motion → software reads sensor value → software turns on light. Students complete or order the steps, or match sensor data types to device behaviors. Auto‑grading checks the flow or matches.
- **CSTA:** E4‑SAS‑HW‑01.

### T30.G4.04 – Troubleshoot sensor or device malfunctions

- **Short name:** Find sensor/device problems
- **Description:** Students identify why a sensor-based system might fail (sensor disconnected, sensor blocked, software not reading sensor, wrong response programmed) and propose fixes. They debug at the hardware–software boundary.
- **Challenge format:** Coding/concept, debugging scenario. Present a broken CreatiCode project or description: "A motion light is supposed to turn on when I move, but it doesn't." Students identify possible causes (sensor not in code, condition is wrong, etc.) and suggest or implement fixes. Auto‑grading checks the diagnostic and fixes applied.
- **CSTA:** E4‑SAS‑HW‑01.

## Grade 5

Grade 5 emphasizes practical troubleshooting steps, data from sensors and hardware, and understanding that many real-world systems combine hardware and software in sophisticated ways.

### T30.G5.01 – Apply troubleshooting steps for hardware/software issues

- **Short name:** Fix common computer problems
- **Description:** Students learn and practice systematic troubleshooting: checking connections, verifying power, restarting devices, checking for updates, freeing storage, and using accurate terminology (USB, battery, plug, restart, reinstall, etc.). They articulate which step to try first and why.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, scenario-based troubleshooting. Present problems: "Computer won't turn on," "Game keeps freezing," "Printer won't print." Students choose or order troubleshooting steps and explain reasoning. Auto‑grading validates selections against known solutions and checks for logical ordering.
- **CSTA:** E5‑SAS‑HW‑01 (Articulate steps to implement common solutions).

### T30.G5.02 – Collect and analyze data from sensors in CreatiCode

- **Short name:** Use sensor data in a program
- **Description:** Students create a CreatiCode project that collects data from simulated sensors (e.g., counting how many times a motion sensor is triggered, averaging temperature readings) and use that data in calculations or decisions. They learn that sensor data becomes variable data.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, starter project. Provided: a simulated sensor (e.g., a sprite that generates random "temperature" readings, or a collision detector). Students create variables to accumulate sensor readings (count, sum) and use the data. Auto‑grading checks that sensor values are collected correctly and used in calculations.
- **CSTA:** E5‑SAS‑HW‑01.

### T30.G5.03 – Understand how software controls embedded systems

- **Short name:** Software in robots and devices
- **Description:** Students understand that embedded systems (robots, smart watches, microcontrollers) run software (firmware, programs) that control their hardware (motors, LEDs, sensors). They can describe how a robot arm, robot vacuum, or drone's program tells the hardware what to do.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, explanation or matching. Show pictures/descriptions of embedded systems (robot, smart watch, drone). Students explain what the software does (controls motors), what the hardware does (moves), and how they work together. Auto‑grading validates explanations against key concepts.
- **CSTA:** E5‑SAS‑HW‑01.

### T30.G5.04 – Design and test a complete sensor system

- **Short name:** Build a sensor system with logic
- **Description:** Students design a more complex CreatiCode system combining multiple sensors, conditional logic, and output (e.g., a security system with motion and door sensors, a simple plant-care system with soil moisture detection, a light controller responding to ambient light and time).
- **Challenge format:** Coding, design challenge. Prompt specifies a real-world system; students implement sensor inputs, conditionals, and outputs. Auto‑grading checks that multiple sensor inputs are simulated, conditions are tested, and outputs respond correctly across test scenarios.
- **CSTA:** E5‑SAS‑HW‑01.

## Grade 6

Middle school (Grade 6) shifts to understanding files, storage, software management, and how systems are organized. Hardware knowledge becomes more about the infrastructure supporting software.

### T30.G6.01 – Describe file systems and storage

- **Short name:** Files, folders, and storage
- **Description:** Students understand that data and programs are stored in files and folders on hardware (hard drives, solid-state drives, cloud servers). They can describe file naming conventions, directory structures, and that storage has limits.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, multiple choice or scenario. Ask about file organization: "Where would you save a project?", "How should you name a file?", "What happens if the drive is full?" Students answer or explain. Auto‑grading validates answers against best practices and concepts.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑HW‑01 (Describe file systems and their role).

### T30.G6.02 – Differentiate web-based and local software

- **Short name:** Cloud apps vs desktop programs
- **Description:** Students distinguish between software that runs locally on a device (offline, stored locally) and web-based apps (run in a browser, data often in the cloud). They understand trade-offs: cloud apps are accessible anywhere but need internet; local apps work offline but are device-specific.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, multiple choice or scenario. Present scenarios: "I want to edit a document offline," "I want to access my photos from any device." Students choose cloud or local software and justify. Auto‑grading checks selections and validates reasoning.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑HW‑02 (Differentiate web-based and local software).

### T30.G6.03 – Apply troubleshooting to identify hardware/software issues

- **Short name:** Diagnose hardware vs software problems
- **Description:** Students apply systematic troubleshooting to determine whether a problem is hardware (device broken, connection loose) or software (app crashed, corrupted file) and suggest appropriate fixes for each type.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, scenario-based troubleshooting. Present complex problems: "Computer is slow," "Internet won't connect," "Video app won't open." Students diagnose the cause (hardware/software) and suggest fixes. Auto‑grading validates diagnoses and proposed solutions.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑HW‑03 (Apply troubleshooting to identify and fix issues).

### T30.G6.04 – Build a simulated embedded device in CreatiCode

- **Short name:** Program a simulated device
- **Description:** Students create a CreatiCode project that simulates an embedded device (robot, smart home controller, game console) with multiple sensors, logic, and outputs responding to sensor data and user input.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, design challenge. Prompt: "Build a simulated [device: robot, smart thermostat, arcade machine] that uses sensors and makes decisions." Students implement sensor reading, conditional logic, and device responses. Auto‑grading tests that the simulated device responds correctly to various inputs and sensor states.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑HW‑01 (implied through system design).

## Grade 7

Grade 7 continues with troubleshooting emphasis and explores more sophisticated system design, including how operating systems manage hardware and software interactions.

### T30.G7.01 – Understand operating systems and system software

- **Short name:** How operating systems work
- **Description:** Students understand that an operating system (Windows, macOS, iOS, Android, Linux) is software that manages hardware resources (CPU, memory, storage, peripherals) and allows other software to run. They can name common operating systems and describe their role in a computing system.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, multiple choice or explanation. Ask: "What is an operating system?", "What does an OS do?", "Name the OS on your device." Students answer or explain. Auto‑grading validates answers.
- **CSTA:** HS‑SAS‑HW‑02 (Differentiate operating systems as special software).

### T30.G7.02 – Diagnose and resolve complex hardware/software issues

- **Short name:** Troubleshoot complex problems
- **Description:** Students apply multi-step troubleshooting to diagnose and resolve complex issues that may involve hardware, software, OS, or user error. They document steps and verify solutions.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, scenario-based troubleshooting. Present complex scenarios: "My project runs on a PC but not on a Mac," "The app crashes on startup," "Network is slow." Students propose systematic diagnostic and repair steps. Auto‑grading validates reasoning and solution strategies.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑HW‑03 (Apply systematic troubleshooting).

### T30.G7.03 – Understand hardware performance and constraints

- **Short name:** Hardware limits and performance
- **Description:** Students understand how hardware specifications (CPU speed, RAM, storage size, screen resolution) affect system performance and capabilities. They can explain why a program might be slow or why a device can't run certain software.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, matching or scenario. Present hardware specs and software needs or performance descriptions. Students match or explain how specs affect performance. Auto‑grading validates connections.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑HW‑01 (implied through understanding system structure).

### T30.G7.04 – Design a more complex simulated system

- **Short name:** Multi-layer system simulation
- **Description:** Students design a CreatiCode simulation of a more complex system (e.g., a home automation system with multiple sensors, user controls, and coordinated outputs; a game with multiple sprites each responding to sensors and user input; a data-collecting weather station).
- **Challenge format:** Coding, design and implementation challenge. Prompt specifies a multi-component system. Students design and code multiple interacting components, each with sensor inputs and logic. Auto‑grading tests component interactions and system behavior.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑HW‑01 (implied through system design).

## Grade 8

Grade 8 deepens understanding of system architecture, troubleshooting sophistication, and how computing devices are designed and used in industry.

### T30.G8.01 – Describe computing devices in industry

- **Short name:** Computers and devices in the real world
- **Description:** Students research and describe computing devices used in various industries (robots in manufacturing, medical imaging devices, electronic control units in vehicles, sensors in agriculture, smart systems in buildings). They explain how each device combines hardware, software, and sensors to accomplish its purpose.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, research and explanation. Students research an industrial device or system and write/present how it works, what hardware is involved, what software controls it, and what sensors it uses. Auto‑grading assesses completeness and accuracy of the explanation.
- **CSTA:** HS‑SAS‑HW‑01 (Describe computing devices in industry).

### T30.G8.02 – Understand how software is deployed and updated

- **Short name:** Software distribution and updates
- **Description:** Students understand that software is developed, tested, packaged, and distributed to devices (app stores, downloads, cloud services), and that updates patch bugs, add features, and improve security. They can explain why updates are important.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, multiple choice or explanation. Ask about software updates: "Why do apps get updates?", "Where do you get a new app?", "What could go wrong if you don't update?" Students answer. Auto‑grading validates understanding.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑HW‑02 (implied through understanding software management).

### T30.G8.03 – Apply advanced troubleshooting and system diagnosis

- **Short name:** Advanced system troubleshooting
- **Description:** Students apply advanced troubleshooting: checking system logs, managing startup programs, diagnosing network issues, testing hardware components, and considering interactions between hardware, OS, and software. They document findings and propose solutions.
- **Challenge format:** Concept/Practical, complex troubleshooting scenario. Present realistic, multi-layered problems (e.g., "Computer is slow and crashes; network keeps dropping"). Students propose diagnostic steps, explain what they would check, and suggest solutions. Auto‑grading validates advanced troubleshooting reasoning.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑HW‑03, HS‑SAS‑HW‑03 (Apply systematic troubleshooting).

### T30.G8.04 – Build a comprehensive system simulation

- **Short name:** Full system design and simulation
- **Description:** Students design and implement a comprehensive CreatiCode simulation that represents a complete system or device (e.g., a smart home with multiple rooms, devices, sensors, and user controls; an autonomous robot with navigation, obstacle detection, and task execution; a data-logging environmental monitor with multiple sensors and visualization).
- **Challenge format:** Coding, capstone design project. Prompt specifies a system to simulate; students design architecture, implement components, integrate inputs/outputs, and test. Auto‑grading evaluates system completeness, correct behavior under test scenarios, and code organization.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑HW‑01, HS‑SAS‑HW‑01 (implied through system design).
