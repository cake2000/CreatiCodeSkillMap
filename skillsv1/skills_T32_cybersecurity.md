# T32 – Cybersecurity & Safe Online Behavior: K–8 Skill List (Draft v1)

Topic reference: `T32 Cybersecurity & Safe Online Behavior` in `domains_topics_overview.md`
Domain: Systems & Security (D5) · CSTA focus: SAS‑SC, CAS‑ET

Each skill below has:

- a stable **ID** (`T32.G<grade>.<nn>`),
- an IXL‑style **short name**,
- a **description** (what understanding/behavior it targets),
- a **challenge format** (the typical problem type to assess it).

Where relevant, a primary **CSTA code** is noted.

---

## Grade K (PreK–K)

Kindergarten introduces cybersecurity as caring for personal information and recognizing trusted adults online.

### T32.GK.01 – Recognize safe vs. unsafe sharing

- **Short name:** Know what not to share online
- **Description:** Students identify types of personal information (name, address, photo, birthdate) and understand that some should not be shared with strangers online. They learn to ask a trusted adult before sharing anything personal.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, multiple choice with pictures. Show a child being asked to share information online (e.g., "Tell me your full name and where you live" from an avatar). Students choose whether it is "safe to share" or "not safe." Auto‑grading checks selections.
- **CSTA:** EK‑SAS‑SC‑02 (Apply practices for keeping personal data secure).

### T32.GK.02 – Tell a trusted adult if something feels wrong

- **Short name:** Know when to tell an adult
- **Description:** Students recognize uncomfortable or confusing online situations (e.g., a stranger saying inappropriate things, or asking to keep secrets) and know to tell a parent, teacher, or trusted adult.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, scenario cards. Show short stories (pictures + text) of online scenarios; students decide "Tell an adult" or "It's okay, keep playing." Auto‑grading checks correct identification of unsafe situations.
- **CSTA:** EK‑SAS‑SC‑02, EK‑CAS‑ET‑02 (Describe how computing technology can help people).

### T32.GK.03 – Match strong password ideas

- **Short name:** Understand what makes a good password (intro)
- **Description:** Students see simple examples of passwords and identify which ones are harder to guess (e.g., "MyDog123" vs "dog" or "123456"). This is pre‑literacy and uses pictures/symbols to represent password strength.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, matching. Show pairs of passwords with visuals indicating strength (e.g., gold star for strong, gray star for weak). Students match passwords to strength labels or click "strong" vs "weak." Auto‑grading verifies choices.
- **CSTA:** EK‑SAS‑SC‑02.

## Grade 1

Grade 1 focuses on understanding that personal information must be kept private and recognizing trustworthy adults online.

### T32.G1.01 – Identify personally identifiable information (PII)

- **Short name:** Know what is private information
- **Description:** Students learn that certain information (full name, address, school, phone number, birthday, photos) is private and should be protected. They recognize examples and non‑examples of PII.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, multiple choice. Show pictures or sentences about different pieces of information; students select which are "private" or "should stay secret." Auto‑grading checks answers against a key.
- **CSTA:** E1‑SAS‑SC‑02 (Evaluate how sharing information online might reveal PII).

### T32.G1.02 – Choose whom to trust online

- **Short name:** Online friends and strangers
- **Description:** Students distinguish between people they know in real life and strangers online, and understand that not all people online are who they say they are. They learn to check with a trusted adult before talking to someone new.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, scenario‑based. Show pictures of online interactions (e.g., message from a friend, message from an unknown person). Students decide "I can talk to them" or "I should ask an adult." Auto‑grading verifies choices.
- **CSTA:** E1‑SAS‑SC‑02.

### T32.G1.03 – Describe why passwords matter

- **Short name:** Passwords keep accounts safe
- **Description:** Students understand that passwords prevent others from accessing their accounts and that they should not share their password even with friends or other students.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, fill‑in‑the‑blank or sentence selection. Prompt: "I should [keep my password secret / share my password]." Or show a picture story where sharing a password leads to account trouble. Auto‑grading checks selected or typed answer.
- **CSTA:** E1‑SAS‑SC‑02.

### T32.G1.04 – Spot a simple online scam picture

- **Short name:** Recognize obvious tricks online
- **Description:** Students see simplified examples of scams (e.g., "You won a prize! Click here!" or "Enter your password to play!") and identify them as "tricks" or "fake."
- **Challenge format:** Concept, multiple choice with cartoons. Show a banner ad or pop‑up; students choose "this is a trick" or "this is real." Auto‑grading verifies.
- **CSTA:** E1‑CAS‑ET‑02 (Describe how technologies new to students create both benefits and harms).

## Grade 2

Grade 2 deepens understanding of personal information protection and introduces basic password strength.

### T32.G2.01 – Explain how to keep information private

- **Short name:** Steps to protect personal data
- **Description:** Students list or order steps for keeping information safe online (e.g., use a strong password, don't share details with strangers, ask a trusted adult). They understand the "why" for each step.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, sequencing or multiple choice. Show a list of actions; students select which ones protect privacy (e.g., "Use a hard-to-guess password" ✓, "Tell your password to friends" ✗). Auto‑grading checks selections.
- **CSTA:** E2‑SAS‑SC‑02 (Explain processes for maintaining secure personal and private information).

### T32.G2.02 – Create a simple strong password

- **Short name:** Build a password with different types of letters
- **Description:** Students create a password using a mix of uppercase, lowercase, and numbers (e.g., "Tiger42Blue"). They learn why mixing types makes passwords harder to guess.
- **Challenge format:** Coding/interactive, password builder tool. CreatiCode or a simple web form allows students to type a password; the system evaluates and provides feedback (e.g., "Good! Has uppercase and numbers"). Auto‑grading checks that the password meets basic criteria (minimum length, variety of character types).
- **CSTA:** E2‑SAS‑SC‑02.

### T32.G2.03 – Recognize a phishing message or fake login

- **Short name:** Spot fake login pages and trick emails
- **Description:** Students see examples of phishing emails or fake login screens and identify them as "fake" or "suspicious." They learn that legitimate sites ask for passwords in specific ways and that unsolicited emails asking for passwords are always tricks.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, multiple choice. Show email screenshots or login form images; students identify which look fake. Auto‑grading verifies choices.
- **CSTA:** E2‑CAS‑ET‑02.

### T32.G2.04 – List safe sharing practices

- **Short name:** Decide what is okay to share online
- **Description:** Students distinguish between safe and unsafe things to share online (e.g., safe: hobby interests, school subject preference; unsafe: home address, phone number, photo without permission).
- **Challenge format:** Concept, categorization. Show a list or cards of things a student might want to share; students sort into "OK to share" and "Not OK to share" categories. Auto‑grading checks placement.
- **CSTA:** E2‑SAS‑SC‑02.

## Grade 3

Grade 3 introduces authentication basics, account security, and reasoning about digital footprints.

### T32.G3.01 – Understand authentication and access levels

- **Short name:** Passwords and permission levels protect accounts
- **Description:** Students learn that authentication (proving you are you via password) prevents unauthorized access, and that different users may have different permissions (e.g., a teacher can edit class documents, a student can only view). They understand the relationship between identity and access.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, scenario‑based. Show a classroom account system where a teacher and student log in with different passwords and see different options. Students answer: "Why can't the student edit grades?" (Answer: they don't have permission because they are not the teacher). Auto‑grading checks reasoning.
- **CSTA:** E3‑SAS‑SC‑03 (Evaluate how authentication, different levels of access, and security measures help protect information).

### T32.G3.02 – Identify and avoid common scams

- **Short name:** Recognize tricks: contests, urgent requests, unknown links
- **Description:** Students recognize common scam patterns: fake prizes or contests, urgent language ("Act now!"), requests from unknown sources, and suspicious links. They learn to verify before clicking or responding.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, multiple choice or true/false. Show message examples (e.g., "Congratulations! You won a free iPad! Click here!" or "Your account needs urgent action—enter your password now"). Students identify "scam" or "safe." Auto‑grading verifies.
- **CSTA:** E3‑SAS‑SC‑03, E3‑CAS‑ET‑02 (Evaluate how people make choices about emerging technologies based on needs and consequences).

### T32.G3.03 – Practice secure account logout

- **Short name:** Log out to keep accounts safe
- **Description:** Students understand that logging out prevents others from accessing an account if they use the same device. They recognize logout buttons and practice using them.
- **Challenge format:** Interactive simulation, step‑by‑step. CreatiCode or web interface shows a logged‑in account screen; students click the "Log Out" button. System responds "You are logged out" and returns to login screen. Auto‑grading checks that logout was performed.
- **CSTA:** E3‑SAS‑SC‑03.

### T32.G3.04 – Understand digital footprint and online reputation

- **Short name:** Know what you leave behind online
- **Description:** Students learn that what they post, comment, or create online stays and can be seen by others. They understand that photos, posts, and messages create a "digital footprint" and can affect how others see them.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, scenario‑based. Show a picture story: student posts a mean comment, takes a silly photo, or shares personal details; the next panel shows someone else seeing it and reacting. Students describe "what happened" or identify the issue. Auto‑grading checks understanding.
- **CSTA:** E3‑CAS‑ET‑02.

## Grade 4

Grade 4 expands understanding of encryption, device security, and privacy settings.

### T32.G4.01 – Encrypt and decrypt a simple message

- **Short name:** Use cipher or code to hide information
- **Description:** Students apply basic encryption (e.g., Caesar cipher, simple substitution) to understand that encryption scrambles information so that only someone with the right "key" can read it. They encrypt and decrypt short messages.
- **Challenge format:** Coding/interactive, cipher tool. CreatiCode project or web form allows students to type a message, apply a simple cipher (e.g., shift by 3), and see the encrypted result. They then decrypt it by shifting back. Auto‑grading verifies correct encryption/decryption.
- **CSTA:** E4‑SAS‑SC‑03 (Model different methods of encryption and decryption for keeping information secure).

### T32.G4.02 – Explain why devices need passwords or biometrics

- **Short name:** Protection for phones and computers
- **Description:** Students understand that devices (phones, tablets, laptops) should be locked with passwords, PINs, or biometrics (fingerprint, face ID) to prevent unauthorized use if the device is lost or stolen.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, reasoning. Scenario: "You left your tablet at a friend's house. Why is it important to have a password on it?" Students choose or write reasons (e.g., "So others can't use it," "To protect your photos and accounts"). Auto‑grading checks reasoning.
- **CSTA:** E4‑SAS‑SC‑03.

### T32.G4.03 – Adjust privacy settings in an app or platform

- **Short name:** Control who sees your profile or posts
- **Description:** Students locate and adjust privacy settings in a simple app or platform (e.g., making a profile private, choosing who can comment, turning off location sharing). They understand that privacy settings give control over personal information.
- **Challenge format:** Interactive tutorial/simulation. A screenshot or screen recording of an app shows privacy settings with checkboxes or toggles. Students click to make a profile "private" or "friends only" and verify the change. Auto‑grading checks that correct setting was applied.
- **CSTA:** E4‑SAS‑SC‑03.

### T32.G4.04 – Identify malware or suspicious software signs

- **Short name:** Spot unwanted programs and warnings
- **Description:** Students recognize signs of malware or adware (unexpected pop‑ups, slow performance, strange toolbars) and know to tell a trusted adult or avoid downloading suspicious programs.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, identification. Show a screen with pop‑ups, slow-running apps, or an unfamiliar toolbar. Students identify "something might be wrong here" and explain why. Auto‑grading checks identification and reasoning.
- **CSTA:** E4‑CAS‑ET‑02 (Investigate intended and unintended consequences related to emerging technologies).

## Grade 5

Grade 5 deepens understanding of multi‑factor authentication, monitoring account activity, and cybersecurity best practices.

### T32.G5.01 – Understand multi‑factor authentication (MFA)

- **Short name:** Two‑step verification for extra security
- **Description:** Students learn that multi‑factor authentication (MFA) requires more than just a password (e.g., password + code from email, password + fingerprint). They understand why this is more secure.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, scenario‑based. Show a login process with MFA: user enters password, system sends a code to email, user must enter the code. Students explain: "Why is this safer than just a password?" or identify it as "more secure." Auto‑grading checks understanding.
- **CSTA:** E5‑SAS‑SC‑03 (Justify the importance of monitoring and updating security measures).

### T32.G5.02 – Monitor account activity for unauthorized access

- **Short name:** Check if your account is being used strangely
- **Description:** Students learn to review login history, connected devices, or recent activity in accounts to detect if someone else has accessed them. They understand what normal activity looks like and what might be suspicious.
- **Challenge format:** Interactive or concept. Show a sample "account activity" screen with login times and locations. Students identify unusual entries (e.g., "Logged in from Tokyo at 3 AM when I'm in California"). They select "This looks wrong" or explain what they notice. Auto‑grading verifies identification.
- **CSTA:** E5‑SAS‑SC‑03.

### T32.G5.03 – Build secure passwords and remember them (or use a manager)

- **Short name:** Create strong, unique passwords
- **Description:** Students create longer, more complex passwords (e.g., with symbols, mixed case, numbers) and learn strategies to remember them (passphrases) or the concept of password managers.
- **Challenge format:** Coding/interactive, password strength checker. Students type a password; the system evaluates complexity and provides feedback (e.g., "Strong," "Very Strong," "Include symbols for extra strength"). Auto‑grading checks that final password meets criteria (length ≥ 12, includes uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols).
- **CSTA:** E5‑SAS‑SC‑03.

### T32.G5.04 – Assess privacy and security of a website or app

- **Short name:** Evaluate before you share information
- **Description:** Students examine a website or app for trust indicators (HTTPS, privacy policy, "Contact Us" page, reviews/ratings) and decide whether it is safe to use or share information with it.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, analysis. Show a website or app page; students identify security features (e.g., "I see a green lock = HTTPS," "It has a privacy policy") and rate trust on a scale or choose "safe" or "not safe yet." Auto‑grading checks reasoning.
- **CSTA:** E5‑CAS‑ET‑02 (Analyze the limitations of existing technologies and how emerging technologies change the way people work, behave, and communicate).

## Grade 6

Middle school deepens cybersecurity awareness with understanding of cyberattacks, data breaches, and responsible online behavior.

### T32.G6.01 – Classify common types of cyberattacks

- **Short name:** Know the main cyberattack types
- **Description:** Students learn to categorize common attacks: phishing, malware, ransomware, DDoS, social engineering. They understand the mechanism of each (e.g., phishing tricks you into revealing info, malware installs unwanted code, ransomware locks files until you pay).
- **Challenge format:** Concept, categorization. Show descriptions or examples of attacks (e.g., "Email claiming to be your bank asks for your password," "Your computer freezes and shows 'Pay $500 to unlock'"). Students match to attack type. Auto‑grading verifies correct classification.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑SC‑07 (Classify common types of cyberattacks, including social engineering and malware).

### T32.G6.02 – Recognize social engineering and manipulation tactics

- **Short name:** Spot tricks to manipulate people
- **Description:** Students identify social engineering tactics: impersonation, pretexting, baiting, tailgating. They understand that attackers manipulate emotions or trust to gain access or information.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, scenario analysis. Show scenarios: "A caller says she's from IT and needs your password," "Someone offers free USB drives in the parking lot," "You get an email from your boss asking for urgent payment." Students identify the tactic and explain why it's a risk. Auto‑grading checks identification and reasoning.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑SC‑07.

### T32.G6.03 – Identify vulnerabilities in computing systems

- **Short name:** Find weaknesses that could be exploited
- **Description:** Students analyze a scenario or system description to identify potential vulnerabilities (e.g., weak password policy, unencrypted data, outdated software, no multi‑factor auth). They understand that vulnerabilities can be exploited by attackers.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, analysis. Describe a scenario (e.g., "A company stores all passwords in plain text in a file"). Students identify the vulnerability and explain what could go wrong. Auto‑grading checks understanding of risk.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑SC‑08 (Evaluate the vulnerabilities of computing systems and data centers).

### T32.G6.04 – Compare cybersecurity and physical security measures

- **Short name:** Understand trade-offs of security measures
- **Description:** Students compare different security approaches (e.g., strong password vs. biometrics, frequent login vs. MFA) and evaluate trade‑offs in convenience, cost, and effectiveness.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, comparison. Present two security measures; students explain pros and cons. E.g., "Using HTTPS is always secure. True or False? Explain." Auto‑grading checks reasoning about trade-offs.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑SC‑09 (Compare and contrast different types of cybersecurity and physical security measures and the trade-offs).

### T32.G6.05 – Understand data breaches and their impact

- **Short name:** Know what happens when data is stolen
- **Description:** Students understand that a data breach exposes personal or sensitive information, and can lead to identity theft, fraud, or privacy violations. They learn responses to breaches (e.g., change passwords, check accounts, monitor credit).
- **Challenge format:** Concept, reasoning. Scenario: "A social media company announces a data breach affecting 1 million users. What could go wrong? What should users do?" Students provide responses. Auto‑grading checks for reasonable concerns and actions.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑SC‑08.

## Grade 7

Grade 7 applies cybersecurity concepts to defense strategies and incident response.

### T32.G7.01 – Explain Defense in Depth strategy

- **Short name:** Layers of security protection
- **Description:** Students understand that security requires multiple layers: network firewalls, device passwords, multi‑factor auth, encryption, and user education. If one layer fails, others still protect. They can describe each layer and why it matters.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, diagram or explanation. Show a system with multiple security layers; students label or describe each layer and its purpose. Or explain what happens if one layer is breached. Auto‑grading checks completeness and accuracy.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑SC‑10 (Describe Defense in Depth strategies and steps to take in response to cybersecurity incidents).

### T32.G7.02 – Respond to a cybersecurity incident

- **Short name:** What to do if you think you've been hacked
- **Description:** Students learn incident response steps: stop using the account, change passwords, check account activity, contact support, scan for malware. They understand the importance of quick action.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, sequencing or multiple choice. Show incident scenarios (e.g., "You get a notification of a login from a city you've never visited"). Students choose or order the correct response steps. Auto‑grading verifies appropriate sequence and actions.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑SC‑10.

### T32.G7.03 – Evaluate how design decisions affect security

- **Short name:** How design choices impact user experience and security
- **Description:** Students analyze how interface design, default settings, or feature choices affect security and user behavior. E.g., "Is it good that sharing is the default? Why?" They understand trade‑offs between ease‑of‑use and security.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, analysis. Present a design choice (e.g., "The app automatically saves passwords"); students evaluate impact on security and convenience. Auto‑grading checks reasoning.
- **CSTA:** MS‑CAS‑ET‑04 (Evaluate how design decisions in emerging technologies influence user experiences).

### T32.G7.04 – Identify secure vs. insecure practices in code or systems

- **Short name:** Spot security flaws in programs
- **Description:** Students review simple code or system descriptions and identify insecure practices (e.g., storing passwords in plain text, not validating user input, hardcoding secrets). They understand why these are risky.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, code analysis. Show a code snippet or pseudocode that has security flaws; students identify the problem (e.g., "This code doesn't check if the password is correct before granting access"). Auto‑grading checks identification of the flaw.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑SC‑08.

## Grade 8

Grade 8 synthesizes cybersecurity understanding into broader contexts and practical decision‑making.

### T32.G8.01 – Analyze real-world cybersecurity incident case studies

- **Short name:** Learn from security breaches and attacks
- **Description:** Students examine documented cybersecurity incidents (e.g., major data breaches, ransomware attacks) to understand how they happened, what vulnerabilities were exploited, and how they could have been prevented or responded to better.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, analysis and writing. Provide a case study summary; students answer: "What vulnerability was exploited? What should have been in place? Who was affected?" Auto‑grading scores written responses for accuracy and completeness.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑SC‑08.

### T32.G8.02 – Make informed decisions about privacy settings and data sharing

- **Short name:** Evaluate privacy trade-offs
- **Description:** Students critically examine when and with whom to share data, considering company policies, their own comfort, and potential risks. They weigh benefits (e.g., personalization, convenience) against risks (tracking, data sales, breaches).
- **Challenge format:** Concept, decision-making. Present a scenario: "A new app offers cool features but asks for location, contacts, and photos. Is it worth it? Why or why not?" Students provide reasoned responses. Auto‑grading checks sophistication of reasoning.
- **CSTA:** MS‑CAS‑ET‑04, MS‑SAS‑SC‑09.

### T32.G8.03 – Understand encryption at a deeper level

- **Short name:** How encryption secures data in transit and at rest
- **Description:** Students understand that encryption protects data both when stored (at rest) and when transmitted (in transit). They distinguish between different encryption contexts (e.g., HTTPS for web, encrypted email, encrypted hard drives) and why each matters.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, explanation or matching. Show scenarios and encryption methods; students match or explain where encryption is used and why. E.g., "Your password should be encrypted when [sent to server / stored on server]." Auto‑grading checks understanding.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑SC‑09.

### T32.G8.04 – Evaluate the security of a technology or system

- **Short name:** Assess security features and risks
- **Description:** Students comprehensively evaluate a technology (app, website, platform, device) for security features, vulnerabilities, privacy practices, and suitability for a particular use. They provide justified recommendations.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, detailed analysis. Provide information about a technology; students complete a rubric or checklist evaluating security, privacy, vulnerability likelihood, and user control. They write a summary recommendation. Auto‑grading uses rubric scoring.
- **CSTA:** MS‑SAS‑SC‑08, MS‑CAS‑ET‑06 (Describe ways emerging technologies have influenced development of new solutions).

### T32.G8.05 – Understand ethical implications of cybersecurity decisions

- **Short name:** Weigh ethics in security trade-offs
- **Description:** Students reflect on ethical questions: Is monitoring employees ethical? Is encryption that prevents police investigation wrong? Are security requirements that exclude some people fair? They practice nuanced reasoning about conflicting values.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, discussion or essay. Present an ethical dilemma (e.g., "A company wants to encrypt customer data, but the government wants a back door to investigate crime. What should the company do? Why?"). Students provide reasoned perspectives. Auto‑grading uses rubric for reasoning quality.
- **CSTA:** MS‑CAS‑ET‑07 (Explain how emerging technologies create new solutions that depend on expertise from multiple fields).
