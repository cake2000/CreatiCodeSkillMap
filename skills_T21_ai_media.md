# T21 – AI Media Tools & Creative Assets: K–8 Skill List (Draft v1)

Topic reference: `T21 AI Media Tools & Creative Assets` in `domains_topics_overview.md`
Domain: CreatiCode-specific AI & Creative Computing · CSTA focus: PRO–PF, CAS–ET (with links to ALG–AF, ALG–HD, ALG–IM, DAA–DI)

Each skill below has:

- a stable **ID** (`T21.G<grade>.<nn>`),
- an IXL‑style **short name**,
- a **description** (what understanding/behavior it targets),
- a **challenge format** (the typical problem type to assess it).

Where relevant, a primary **CSTA code** is noted.

---

## Grade K (PreK–K)

Grade K introduces students to the concept that AI can create and modify artwork, and that computers can help make pictures and backdrops for their stories.

### T21.GK.01 – Recognize that AI can create pictures

- **Short name:** AI can make pictures
- **Description:** Students understand that artificial intelligence is a tool that can generate images from text descriptions. They recognize that AI-created pictures are different from hand-drawn or photographed images, but equally useful for creative projects. This builds foundational awareness of AI as a creative assistant.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, interactive exploration. The teacher shows a backdrop or sprite costume that was generated by AI, then asks students "How do you think the computer made this picture?" Students select from options like "a person drew it," "AI made it from words," or "a photo." Auto‑grading checks understanding.
- **CSTA:** CAS–ET–02 (Emerging technologies used in daily life).

### T21.GK.02 – Choose between AI-generated and hand-drawn art

- **Short name:** Pick which picture is AI-made
- **Description:** Students distinguish between AI-generated images and hand-drawn or photographic images by examining style, detail, and artificiality. This develops visual literacy and prepares them to make intentional choices about which type of art suits their story.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, multiple choice. Show 3–4 pictures of sprites or backdrops; students identify which was made by AI and which were drawn or photographed. Hint: AI-generated images often have characteristic blending or slight irregularities. Auto‑grading compares to reference answers.
- **CSTA:** CAS–ET–02.

### T21.GK.03 – Describe what a picture shows in simple words

- **Short name:** Tell what you see in a picture
- **Description:** Students use simple language to describe the content, colors, and actions in artwork (both AI-generated and human-created). This develops the ability to articulate visual concepts, which is the foundation for later writing AI image prompts.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, guided response. An AI-generated sprite or backdrop is shown; students complete sentence stems like "This picture shows a _______ that is _______." Auto‑grading checks for appropriate descriptive words and simple complete thoughts.
- **CSTA:** ALG–AF–01 (Recognize patterns; early language development).

## Grade 1

Grade 1 students explore the idea that detailed descriptions lead to better pictures, and they begin to use the AI image search feature in CreatiCode.

### T21.G1.01 – Search for AI-generated pictures in CreatiCode

- **Short name:** Find pictures using words
- **Description:** Students use the CreatiCode AI Image Library to search for backdrop or sprite images by typing words (e.g., "forest," "robot," "sunny day"). They learn that typing good search words helps find the right picture. This introduces basic retrieval and the concept of human-computer communication through language.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, guided project. Starter project includes a prompt: "Find a backdrop for your story about [topic]." Students click the AI button, type a search word, and select a suitable image from results. Auto‑grading checks that an image was retrieved and applied correctly.
- **CSTA:** PRO–PF–02 (Create programs; early data/input understanding).

### T21.G1.02 – Describe what you want in a picture

- **Short name:** Use words to describe your idea
- **Description:** Students write or speak 2–4 descriptive words to say what kind of picture they want (e.g., "big red house" or "happy dancing cat"). This builds the foundation for prompt writing without yet requiring the students to formally generate images.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, creative expression. Students are given a scene or character idea and asked to provide descriptive words orally or with picture supports. A helper can scribe or check their word choices. Auto‑grading is manual, focusing on whether the words relate to the concept.
- **CSTA:** ALG–AF–01.

### T21.G1.03 – Understand that better words make better pictures

- **Short name:** More details help the AI
- **Description:** Students compare two search attempts or two AI-generated images: one from a simple search ("cat") and one from a more detailed search ("fluffy orange cat with white spots"). They notice that more specific words lead to results closer to what they wanted. This introduces the concept of prompt engineering at a developmentally appropriate level.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, side-by-side comparison. Show two versions of a search (or generated image) with different prompt details. Ask students which one is closer to the intended idea, and why. Auto‑grading checks reasoning.
- **CSTA:** ALG–AF–01, CAS–ET–02.

### T21.G1.04 – Add an AI-generated picture to a simple story project

- **Short name:** Use AI art in a story
- **Description:** Students create a simple story (2–3 scenes) where at least one sprite or backdrop is found or generated using CreatiCode's AI tools. They combine AI-generated assets with their own ideas to tell a story, understanding that AI images are creative resources.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, creative project. Starter project includes sprites and a basic storyline. Students search for or are guided to generate one new backdrop or costume using AI, then incorporate it into their story. Auto‑grading checks that an AI image was used and is appropriate to the context.
- **CSTA:** PRO–PF–02, ALG–HD–02 (Design improvements; introduction to AI as tool).

## Grade 2

Grade 2 introduces formal AI image generation in CreatiCode, refining prompts to get desired results, and understanding responsible use of AI art.

### T21.G2.01 – Write a prompt to generate an AI picture

- **Short name:** Generate an image from a description
- **Description:** Students write a text prompt (5–10 words describing what image they want) and use CreatiCode's AI Image Generator to create a sprite or backdrop. They learn that a clear, detailed description yields better results. This is the first formal practice in prompt engineering.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, starter project with prompt guidance. Provided: empty sprite/backdrop slots and a prompt template like "A [adjective] [noun] that is [action/state]." Students fill in the template and click Generate. Auto‑grading checks (1) that the prompt was submitted, (2) an image was generated, and (3) basic appropriateness.
- **CSTA:** PRO–PF–02, ALG–HD–02.

### T21.G2.02 – Choose the right model type (Human vs Non-Human vs Vector)

- **Short name:** Pick the right AI model for your image
- **Description:** Students understand that CreatiCode offers different AI models: Human (for people/characters), Non-Human (for animals/objects), and Vector (for illustration-style art). They select the appropriate model for their creative intent. This introduces the idea that different AI tools are suited to different tasks.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, guided selection. Scenarios are presented: "I want a robot holding a wrench. Which model should I choose?" or "I want a watercolor landscape. Which?" Students select the model and justify briefly. Auto‑grading checks correct selection and reasoning.
- **CSTA:** ALG–AF–01, CAS–ET–02 (Emerging technologies have different characteristics).

### T21.G2.03 – Regenerate and refine an AI image

- **Short name:** Try again if the picture isn't right
- **Description:** Students learn that if an AI-generated image doesn't match their vision, they can edit their prompt and generate a new version. They iterate on their description to get closer to their goal, practicing persistence and refinement.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, iterative project. Starter project: generate an initial image, compare to a target image shown on screen, then edit the prompt and regenerate. Students do this 2–3 times. Auto‑grading checks that multiple generations occurred and final image is closer to target.
- **CSTA:** PRO–TR–03 (Debug and refine; iterative improvement).

### T21.G2.04 – Combine AI-generated and hand-drawn art in one project

- **Short name:** Mix AI and drawn pictures
- **Description:** Students create a project that includes both AI-generated assets (backdrop or character) and hand-drawn or built-in Scratch assets. They understand that different media can work together and may choose whichever is best for their story or game.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, creative project. Prompt: "Make a scene with one AI-generated element and one Scratch element (e.g., a painted sprite or built-in costume)." Students generate/find one AI image and incorporate a hand-drawn or classic Scratch sprite. Auto‑grading checks for presence of both types of assets.
- **CSTA:** ALG–AF–01, ALG–IM–04 (Different approaches; responsible choice).

## Grade 3

Grade 3 deepens prompt engineering skills, introduces ethical considerations about AI art, and explores combining AI images with drawing tools and animation.

### T21.G3.01 – Write detailed prompts with style and mood words

- **Short name:** Use style words in AI prompts
- **Description:** Students expand their prompt vocabulary to include style descriptors ("watercolor," "cartoon," "realistic," "pixel art") and mood words ("spooky," "joyful," "mysterious"). They learn that these words significantly influence the generated image's aesthetic.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, guided generation. Provided: a list of style/mood words with visual examples. Prompt: "Generate a [noun] that looks [style] and feels [mood]." Students select appropriate words and generate. Auto‑grading checks vocabulary use and visual appropriateness.
- **CSTA:** ALG–AF–01, CAS–ET–02.

### T21.G3.02 – Understand that AI training data affects what it can create

- **Short name:** What an AI learns from data
- **Description:** Students learn that AI image generators are trained on many examples of images, so they can only make pictures of things they've "seen" in training. They understand that biases in training data can affect what the AI creates, introducing early ethical thinking.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, discussion and observation. Teacher shows examples: successful generation of a "dragon" vs difficulty generating a "unicorn on a skateboard." Students discuss why some images are easier for AI to create. Auto‑grading is manual (discussion notes or short written reflection).
- **CSTA:** CAS–ET–02, ALG–IM–04 (Algorithms and their impacts).

### T21.G3.03 – Search for and remix existing community AI art

- **Short name:** Find community art and make it yours
- **Description:** Students search the CreatiCode AI Image Library for existing community-generated images that suit their project. They use library images as starting points, selecting and adapting them for their story or game.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, project-based. Prompt: "Find at least two AI-generated images from the library that would work for your scene." Students search using descriptive terms and select images. Auto‑grading checks that library images were discovered and used (vs. only newly generated).
- **CSTA:** PRO–PF–02, ALG–HD–02.

### T21.G3.04 – Give credit when using AI images (attribution)

- **Short name:** Say where your pictures came from
- **Description:** Students understand that when they use AI-generated images in a project, they should acknowledge that AI was used to create them. They practice adding comments or credits in their projects (e.g., "Backdrop made with CreatiCode AI") to be honest and transparent.
- **Challenge format:** Concept + coding, project documentation. Starter project includes a comment block or label widget. Prompt: "Add a comment or label that says 'Made with CreatiCode AI' or 'Backdrop by AI.'" Auto‑grading checks for presence of attribution in code or project metadata.
- **CSTA:** PRO–PM–05 (Documentation; intellectual honesty).

## Grade 4

Grade 4 students explore AI image editing, combining pen/drawing tools with AI images, and critically evaluating ethical questions about AI-generated art.

### T21.G4.01 – Edit an AI image with the pen tool

- **Short name:** Draw over or modify an AI picture
- **Description:** Students take an AI-generated image (used as a backdrop costume) and use CreatiCode's pen or drawing tools to add their own marks, details, or corrections. This blends human and AI creativity in a concrete way.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, creative challenge with media. Starter project provides an AI-generated backdrop in costume slot. Students use pen blocks to draw additional details (e.g., draw a door on an AI house, or add stars to an AI sky). Auto‑grading checks that pen drawing occurred on top of the image.
- **CSTA:** ALG–AF–01, ALG–HD–02 (Combining and refining).

### T21.G4.02 – Compare AI-generated vs. hand-drawn versions of the same idea

- **Short name:** AI vs. human art: differences and choices
- **Description:** Students generate an AI image for a given prompt, then observe or attempt a hand-drawn version (or compare to a reference image). They discuss which better suits the project's needs, mood, or artistic goals. This develops critical thinking about tool selection.
- **Challenge format:** Concept + creative, comparative analysis. Prompt: "Generate an AI [object]. Find or create a hand-drawn version. Which works better for a [mood/game type]? Why?" Students justify their choice in a written reflection. Auto‑grading checks reasoning quality.
- **CSTA:** CAS–ET–02, ALG–IM–04 (Different tools, different impacts).

### T21.G4.03 – Evaluate an AI image for bias or appropriateness

- **Short name:** Check an AI image for fairness and safety
- **Description:** Students examine AI-generated images to identify potential biases (e.g., whether certain character types are represented fairly) or appropriateness issues (violence, stereotypes). They practice critical evaluation of AI outputs before using them.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, guided evaluation. Shown: a set of 3–4 AI-generated character or scene images. Prompt: "Do you see any biases? Is this picture appropriate? Would you want to use it?" Students justify. Auto‑grading is manual but structured (checklist: considers representation, appropriateness, respectfulness).
- **CSTA:** ALG–IM–04, CAS–ET–02 (Impacts of emerging technology).

### T21.G4.04 – Write clear, unbiased prompts for AI image generation

- **Short name:** Make fair prompts for the AI
- **Description:** Students learn to write prompts that don't reinforce stereotypes or biases. For example, instead of "a doctor" (which may unconsciously bias toward certain demographics), they might write "a diverse team of doctors" or just "a person in medical clothing." This practices ethical AI use.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, guided practice. Prompt sets are provided. Students are asked to rewrite biased or vague prompts to be more inclusive: "a scientist" becomes "people of different backgrounds working in a lab." Auto‑grading checks for inclusion and fairness language.
- **CSTA:** ALG–HD–02, ALG–IM–04 (Design for equity; impacts of algorithms).

## Grade 5

Grade 5 students master advanced prompt engineering, document their creative process with AI, and explore the responsible use and copyright considerations of AI-generated art.

### T21.G5.01 – Write a detailed visual specification as a prompt

- **Short name:** Describe a complex scene with precise words
- **Description:** Students write longer, more precise prompts (15–20+ words) that specify composition, colors, lighting, perspective, and emotional tone. For example: "A sunny day in a forest clearing, with tall pine trees, a small wooden cabin in the distance, soft golden light filtering through leaves, warm and peaceful mood." This represents advanced prompt engineering.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, creative challenge. Starter project: a visual target image or detailed scene description. Students write a long, detailed prompt and generate an image. Auto‑grading compares generated image to target using similarity metrics and manual review of prompt clarity.
- **CSTA:** ALG–AF–01, ALG–HD–02 (Advanced design with detail).

### T21.G5.02 – Experiment with prompt variations to understand AI responses

- **Short name:** Try different prompts and compare results
- **Description:** Students systematically generate multiple images from slight prompt variations (e.g., changing adjectives, adding/removing details) to understand how specific words influence output. This develops scientific thinking about AI behavior.
- **Challenge format:** Concept + coding, experiment design. Prompt: "Change one word in your prompt each time and generate 3 versions. What changed in the pictures?" Students create a simple log documenting changes and observe results. Auto‑grading checks that multiple systematic variations were attempted and observations recorded.
- **CSTA:** ALG–PS–03 (Analyzing and decomposing), CAS–ET–02.

### T21.G5.03 – Document and explain AI art choices in a project

- **Short name:** Write about how you used AI in your project
- **Description:** Students create a written or recorded reflection explaining which images they generated, why, what prompts they used, and how the AI images fit their creative vision. This develops metacognition and documentation skills.
- **Challenge format:** Coding + documentation, project reflection. After a creative project is complete, students write or record: "I generated [this image] using the prompt: '[prompt].' I chose this because…" Auto‑grading checks completeness and clarity of explanation.
- **CSTA:** PRO–PM–05 (Documentation).

### T21.G5.04 – Understand copyright and responsible use of AI-generated art

- **Short name:** Know the rules about using AI pictures
- **Description:** Students learn that CreatiCode-generated images are owned by the platform and can only be used in CreatiCode projects. They understand that they should not claim AI images as their own and should credit the tool. This introduces intellectual property and digital citizenship.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, case studies and discussion. Presented scenarios: "Can I use my AI-generated backdrop in a printed poster?" or "Should I tell my friend that a picture is AI-made?" Students discuss and explain. Auto‑grading checks understanding of platform terms and honesty.
- **CSTA:** PRO–PM–05, CAS–ET–02 (Responsible use of emerging technologies).

## Grade 6

In middle school, AI media tool use becomes more sophisticated. Students explore how AI-generated assets fit into larger game and animation design, and they consider systemic questions about AI in creative work.

### T21.G6.01 – Plan a project that uses AI-generated, hand-drawn, and remix assets

- **Short name:** Design an asset strategy for a project
- **Description:** Students plan which assets (sprite costumes, backdrops, animations) will be AI-generated, which will be hand-drawn, and which will use existing/remixed Scratch library elements. They justify each choice based on efficiency, artistic goals, and project scope.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, project design document. Prompt: "Plan a simple game/story. For each major asset, decide: AI-generated, hand-drawn, or remix? Explain why." Students fill in a planning table. Auto‑grading checks thoughtfulness and completeness.
- **CSTA:** ALG–AF–01, ALG–HD–03 (Design with user needs; human-centered).

### T21.G6.02 – Use AI-generated images with prompt variations to create animation

- **Short name:** Generate multiple poses for animation
- **Description:** Students generate multiple AI images by varying prompts slightly (e.g., "person standing," "person running," "person jumping") and use them as animation frames. They understand how AI can speed up asset creation for animation.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, animation project. Prompt: "Generate at least 4 AI images showing the same character in different poses/actions. Import them as costumes and create a simple animation." Auto‑grading checks that (1) multiple AI images were generated, (2) they're used as costumes, and (3) animation occurs.
- **CSTA:** PRO–PF–01, ALG–HD–02.

### T21.G6.03 – Analyze the impact of AI-generated assets on game/story authenticity

- **Short name:** Think about AI's role in your creative vision
- **Description:** Students examine games or stories that use AI-generated art and discuss: Does the AI art fit the style? Does it feel authentic to the creator's vision? Would hand-drawn art change the feeling? This develops critical media literacy.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, analysis essay. Students play/read a project with AI assets, then write or discuss: "How do the AI images make this project feel? Would it be different if they were drawn by hand?" Auto‑grading checks depth of analysis.
- **CSTA:** CAS–ET–02, ALG–IM–08 (Impacts of technologies on people and society).

### T21.G6.04 – Troubleshoot a failed AI generation and revise the prompt

- **Short name:** Fix a bad AI image by rewriting the prompt
- **Description:** Students are shown a failed or unsuitable AI-generated image and asked to diagnose the problem by analyzing the original prompt. They revise and regenerate, practicing debugging and iteration in a creative context.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, debugging task. Provided: a starter project with an unsatisfactory AI image and the original prompt (e.g., "blue cat" generated a strange abstract shape). Students identify the issue, revise the prompt (e.g., "a blue cat sitting on a chair"), and regenerate. Auto‑grading checks that the new image is more suitable.
- **CSTA:** PRO–TR–11 (Debugging; systematic problem-solving).

## Grade 7

Grade 7 students explore the technical and societal implications of AI in creative media, including limitations of AI, bias in training data, and the future of AI-assisted creativity.

### T21.G7.01 – Explain why AI struggles with certain image types

- **Short name:** Understand AI image generation limits
- **Description:** Students learn that AI image generators struggle with specific subjects: hands (often distorted), text (misspelled), complex mathematical patterns, or specific cultural imagery. They understand these limitations are due to training data gaps and model architecture, not lack of intelligence.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, exploration and discussion. Prompt: "Try to generate [hand-heavy image] or [text-heavy image]. What goes wrong? Why might the AI struggle with this?" Students experiment and discuss causes. Auto‑grading is manual (participation and reasoning quality).
- **CSTA:** CAS–ET–02, ALG–IM–08.

### T21.G7.02 – Examine bias in AI-generated images and discuss causes

- **Short name:** Recognize and discuss bias in AI art
- **Description:** Students systematically test AI image generators with prompts that should yield diverse results (e.g., "a doctor," "a teacher," "a CEO"). They observe whether the AI perpetuates stereotypes based on gender, race, age, or other factors, and discuss why training data bias causes this.
- **Challenge format:** Concept + coding, investigative project. Students generate images with identical prompts from different "demographics" and compare (e.g., "a young doctor" vs "an old doctor" vs "a doctor" alone). They analyze patterns and write reflections on bias. Auto‑grading checks systematic approach and thoughtfulness.
- **CSTA:** ALG–IM–08, DAA–IM–14 (Bias in data and algorithms).

### T21.G7.03 – Plan and execute an AI-heavy creative project with intentional choices

- **Short name:** Lead a major project using AI media
- **Description:** Students design and build a substantial game or multimedia story (8+ screens/scenes) where AI-generated images are a primary asset source. They make intentional decisions about prompts, style consistency, and ethical considerations throughout the project.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, capstone-style project. Students propose a game or interactive story, specify how many AI assets they'll generate, write detailed prompts, and build the project. Auto‑grading checks (1) scale and complexity, (2) quality of prompts, (3) integration and appropriateness of AI assets, and (4) evidence of thoughtful planning.
- **CSTA:** ALG–HD–04, PRO–PM–16 (Documentation, planning, intentional design).

### T21.G7.04 – Compare creative workflows with and without AI tools

- **Short name:** Compare hand-drawn vs. AI-assisted workflows
- **Description:** Students complete two similar creative tasks—one using only hand-drawn/remix assets and one using AI generation—and reflect on differences in time, effort, quality, and artistic satisfaction. This builds nuanced thinking about tool tradeoffs.
- **Challenge format:** Concept + coding, comparative project. Prompt: "Make a simple scene two ways: (1) draw everything yourself, (2) use AI for at least 50% of assets. Compare: time, effort, fun, final quality." Students document and reflect. Auto‑grading checks thoughtfulness of comparison.
- **CSTA:** CAS–ET–02, ALG–IM–08.

## Grade 8

Grade 8 students investigate ethical questions about AI authorship, ownership, and the future of AI in creative industries, and they explore advanced techniques like style transfer and the role of AI in professional creative work.

### T21.G8.01 – Investigate questions of authorship and credit in AI-assisted art

- **Short name:** Who made the art: human or AI?
- **Description:** Students explore the philosophical question: When a human writes an AI prompt and the AI generates an image, who is the "artist"? They research real-world debates (e.g., AI art competitions, copyright disputes) and form informed opinions on credit and ownership.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, research and essay. Prompt: "Research a real case where someone used AI to create art. Who gets credit? Is it fair? What do you think?" Students write a short evidence-based reflection. Auto‑grading checks research quality and reasoned argument.
- **CSTA:** CAS–ET–02, CAS–HC–01 (Understanding emerging technologies and their societal role).

### T21.G8.02 – Analyze how AI image generation might change creative industries

- **Short name:** How will AI change art, animation, and game design?
- **Description:** Students examine how professional game studios, animators, and illustrators are (or might) adopt AI tools. They consider both opportunities (faster prototyping, accessibility) and concerns (job displacement, cultural homogenization). This develops systems thinking about technology and society.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, research and discussion. Prompt: "Interview (or research) how a game studio or illustrator might use AI. What are the benefits and risks?" Students present findings and opinion. Auto‑grading checks evidence gathering and balanced analysis.
- **CSTA:** CAS–ET–02, CAS–HC–03 (Historical context; future impacts).

### T21.G8.03 – Design an AI-assisted creative workflow for a realistic scenario

- **Short name:** Plan an AI workflow for a game or film studio
- **Description:** Students design a realistic workflow for a small indie game or animation team using AI image generation for concept art, backgrounds, or character variations. They consider quality, consistency, revision cycles, and team roles. This applies AI tool knowledge to authentic professional contexts.
- **Challenge format:** Coding + planning, professional simulation. Prompt: "You're a small team making a fantasy RPG with limited time. How will you use AI to speed up art creation while maintaining quality?" Students create a workflow document with prompts, iteration plans, and roles. Auto‑grading checks feasibility and professionalism of the plan.
- **CSTA:** ALG–HD–03, CAS–CE–11 (Career exploration; professional practice).

### T21.G8.04 – Evaluate the ethical implications of large-scale AI deployment in creative fields

- **Short name:** Is it ethical to use AI in creative industries?
- **Description:** Students evaluate multi-faceted ethical questions: Should creators be required to disclose AI use? Does AI democratize creativity or homogenize it? What happens to artists whose work was used to train AI? They form nuanced positions informed by multiple perspectives.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, debate and position paper. Prompt: "Take a position: Is AI image generation good or bad for the creative world? Use evidence and consider multiple perspectives." Students write or argue their position. Auto‑grading checks evidence quality, fairness to opposing views, and reasoning rigor.
- **CSTA:** CAS–ET–02, ALG–IM–08 (Impacts of algorithms and emerging technologies).
