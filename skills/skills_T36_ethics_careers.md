Each skill below has:

- a stable **ID** (`T36.G<grade>.<nn>`),

## Grade K (PreK–K)

Kindergarten focuses on recognizing that technology and digital tools help people in different jobs and identifying the basic value of kindness and sharing in working together.

### T36.GK.01 – People use digital tools in their jobs

- **Short name:** Match workers to their digital tools
- **Description:** Students recognize that people in different jobs (teacher, doctor, farmer, artist, chef) use digital tools and devices to do their work. They understand that computers are helpful in many kinds of work, not just "computer jobs."
- **Challenge format:** Concept, matching activity. Show picture cards of workers (teacher, doctor, firefighter, artist) and digital tool cards (laptop, tablet, robot, digital camera). Students match workers to the tools they might use. Auto‑grading checks correct matches.
- **CSTA:** EK‑CAS‑CE‑03 (Identify how people use digital devices in their daily work).

### T36.GK.02 – Kindness when sharing and taking turns

- **Short name:** Tell how to be kind in group work
- **Description:** Students identify and practice simple acts of kindness and fairness when sharing tools, taking turns with computers or blocks, and listening to classmates. They develop basic understanding that working together feels better when everyone is respectful.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, interactive story or scenario. Short scenarios show two children working on a project together. One version shows unkind behavior (grabbing, ignoring), another shows kindness (taking turns, listening). Students select the kind version or identify what makes it better. Auto‑grading checks their choices.
- **CSTA:** CAS‑ET (respect and inclusion foundations), PRO‑PM (collaboration fundamentals).

### T36.GK.03 – How does this tool help people?

- **Short name:** Explain how a tool is helpful
- **Description:** Students observe or use a simple digital tool (drawing app, voice recorder, animation app) and explain in simple terms how it helps people do their work or have fun. They begin to connect "usefulness" to the idea of technology's positive impact.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, open response with pictures. Prompt: "We use [tool] to [purpose]. How does it help?" or show a picture of someone using a tool and ask "What good thing is happening here?" Auto‑grading uses keyword/concept matching (e.g., "make," "help," "fun," "learn").
- **CSTA:** EK‑CAS‑ET‑02 (Describe how computing technology used in daily life can help people).

## Grade 1

Grade 1 extends recognition of technology in careers to include a wider range of jobs and introduces the idea that technology can bring both benefits and harm.

### T36.G1.01 – Many different jobs use computers

- **Short name:** List jobs that use computers
- **Description:** Students name or recognize a variety of jobs and professions (scientists, teachers, artists, musicians, builders, store workers) that use computing technology in their work. They expand their awareness beyond typical "computer" jobs to see computing is everywhere.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, multiple choice or list creation. Show or describe 5–6 professions; students select all that use computers or computers/digital tools in their work (some distractors included). Auto‑grading checks all correct selections and no incorrect ones.
- **CSTA:** E1‑CAS‑CE‑03 (Describe how computing is used in a variety of industries and careers).

### T36.G1.02 – Technology brings both good things and problems

- **Short name:** Identify benefits and harms of a technology
- **Description:** Students observe examples of a technology (smartphone, video game, social media, educational app) in action and recognize that while it helps people, it can also cause problems (screen time, unfairness, lost playtime). They begin to think about tradeoffs.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, two‑column sorting or scenario pairs. Show a scenario with a technology (e.g., "A video game helps kids learn math AND kids play for hours and don't sleep"). Students select or sort phrases into "good things" and "problems." Auto‑grading checks categorization.
- **CSTA:** E1‑CAS‑ET‑02 (Describe how technologies new to students create both benefits and harms in personal and family life).

### T36.G1.03 – Listening is important in group work

- **Short name:** Show you are listening to your team
- **Description:** Students practice active listening when peers explain ideas—making eye contact, waiting their turn, and responding appropriately. They understand that listening helps a group work together better.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, video observation and selection. Show short videos of students working in pairs; one pair shows good listening (nodding, waiting, asking questions), another shows poor listening (interrupting, looking away). Students identify the better example and explain why. Auto‑grading checks selected choice and keyword use.
- **CSTA:** PRO‑PM (Inclusive Collaboration pillar: communicate clearly and listen).

### T36.G1.04 – Share your ideas and ask questions

- **Short name:** Speak up and ask in group projects
- **Description:** Students gain confidence in speaking their ideas and asking questions during group work, even if they are unsure. They learn that everyone's ideas matter and that asking "Why?" or "How?" helps the group.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, role‑play or guided dialogue. Scenarios show a student hesitant to share an idea, then sharing it and the group responding positively. Students practice saying "I think..." or "Can we try...?" with visual/audio support. Auto‑grading uses speech recognition or teacher checklist.
- **CSTA:** PRO‑PM (Inclusive Collaboration: communicate clearly using oral methods).

## Grade 2

Grade 2 introduces the idea that new and improved technologies can solve problems, encourages reflection on how to work fairly and respectfully, and begins to explore the relationship between ideas and outcomes.

### T36.G2.01 – New tech to solve a problem

- **Short name:** Imagine a technology that solves a problem
- **Description:** Students observe a simple problem (kids lose toys, hard to find things, need to stay warm) and hypothesize how a new or improved technology could help solve it. They develop the habit of thinking "How could a tool or computer help?"
- **Challenge format:** Concept, open response with prompts. Prompt: "If kids always lose their water bottles, what technology could help?" Students write or draw a simple solution (like a tracker, a labeled bottle, a reminder app). Auto‑grading uses rubric scoring for creativity and relevance; keyword matching for expected ideas.
- **CSTA:** E2‑CAS‑ET‑02 (Hypothesize ways that new or improved technologies can solve a problem).

### T36.G2.02 – Everyone gets a turn and a voice

- **Short name:** Make sure all voices are heard
- **Description:** Students practice strategies to include everyone in group work: asking quiet members for input, rotating who speaks first, taking turns with the computer, making time for different ideas. They experience or observe groups where everyone contributes.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, scenario and planning. Show a group project with one person dominating; students offer suggestions to include others (e.g., "Ask Jade what she thinks," "Let Sam be the next driver"). Auto‑grading checks for inclusion and fairness ideas using keywords.
- **CSTA:** PRO‑PM (Inclusive Collaboration: manage project teams fairly), CAS‑ET (respect and inclusion).

### T36.G2.03 – Tell what went well and what to improve

- **Description:** Students reflect on a group project or collaboration and identify what worked well and what could be better next time. They practice giving and receiving friendly feedback.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, structured reflection. After a short collaborative task in CreatiCode, students complete a simple form: "One thing we did well: _____. One thing to improve: _____." Auto‑grading checks for specific, constructive feedback using keywords.
- **CSTA:** PRO‑PM (Inclusive Collaboration: reflection on collaborative work).

### T36.G2.04 – Show your project to others

- **Short name:** Present a project to the class
- **Description:** Students prepare to share a completed CreatiCode project with peers or teachers, learning to explain what their project does, what they built, and what they learned. This is a first step toward project communication.
- **Challenge format:** Coding and communication, presentation. After completing a simple CreatiCode project (animation, game, or story), students prepare a 1-minute "pitch": "This project is about _____. I built _____. I learned _____." Auto‑grading uses a rubric (clarity, completeness, confidence) based on video recording or teacher observation.
- **CSTA:** PRO‑PM (Communicate about your project).

## Grade 3

Grade 3 deepens reflection on how people make choices about technology, introduces the concept of fairness in algorithms, and emphasizes respectful collaboration and project documentation.

### T36.G3.01 – Why do people choose to use (or not use) a technology?

- **Short name:** Explain a choice about using technology
- **Description:** Students analyze real or hypothetical scenarios where a person chooses whether to use a technology (a smartphone, a gaming console, AI tool, video call) and explain the reasons: need, interest, consequences. They explore the idea that our choices about technology affect ourselves and others.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, analysis and written response. Scenario: "Your family wants to know if you should have a social media account. What are good reasons to do it? What are reasons to wait?" Students list benefits and concerns, then explain their recommendation. Auto‑grading uses rubric with keywords for "needs," "consequences," "impact on others."
- **CSTA:** E3‑CAS‑ET‑02 (Evaluate how people make choices about the use of emerging technologies based on their needs and consequences).

### T36.G3.02 – Fair and unfair in algorithms

- **Short name:** Spot unfairness in how a computer decides
- **Description:** Students recognize that a computer program (algorithm) can treat people differently or unfairly if not designed carefully. For example, a recommendation engine might only suggest certain types of games to boys or girls, or a filter might miss some faces. They begin to think critically about algorithm fairness.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, scenario analysis. Show scenarios: a robot that rejects some students' artwork unfairly, a recommendation system that excludes certain groups, a game that is harder for some players without reason. Students identify the unfairness and suggest how to fix it. Auto‑grading checks for identification of unfairness and a reasonable fix (e.g., "Include all types of artwork," "Make the game difficulty same for everyone").
- **CSTA:** ALG‑IM (Impacts of Algorithms: fairness and bias), CAS‑ET (ethics and impacts).

### T36.G3.03 – Document your project so others understand

- **Short name:** Write comments to explain your code
- **Description:** Students add simple comments or descriptions to their CreatiCode projects so that someone else (a classmate, teacher, or future self) can understand what each part does. They learn that clear communication improves the quality of their work.
- **Challenge format:** Coding, documentation task. Starter project is given with several unlabeled sections; students add comment blocks or sprite descriptions explaining "This sprite is the player," "This loop makes the enemy move," "This if statement checks if we won." Auto‑grading checks for presence and relevance of comments using keyword/phrase matching.
- **CSTA:** PRO‑PM (Communicate about your project; documentation).

### T36.G3.04 – Work together fairly: roles and responsibilities

- **Short name:** Assign and explain team roles
- **Description:** Students practice assigning roles in a group project (idea person, builder, tester, presenter) so that everyone has a job and knows what they are responsible for. They understand that clear roles reduce conflict and help the team succeed.
- **Challenge format:** Concept and planning, role assignment. Before a group project, students select or are assigned a role card (Designer, Coder, Artist, Tester). They explain their role and what they will do. Auto‑grading uses a checklist: each student has a role, roles are distinct, and students can explain their responsibilities using provided sentence stems.
- **CSTA:** PRO‑PM (Actively manage projects and project teams).

## Grade 4

Grade 4 deepens investigation of technology's intended and unintended consequences, introduces the concept of bias in data and algorithms, and strengthens project communication and ethical reasoning.

### T36.G4.01 – Intended and unintended consequences

- **Short name:** Predict good and bad effects of a technology
- **Description:** Students investigate how a technology (social media, educational software, automation, AI tool) was designed to help people but can also cause unintended harm: making people lonely, spreading misinformation, losing jobs, violating privacy. They practice systems thinking—understanding that one change can ripple.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, extended analysis. Scenario: "A company creates an app to help friends stay in touch (intended: friendship). What bad things might happen?" Students brainstorm 2–3 unintended consequences (addiction, cyberbullying, privacy loss) and suggest a way to reduce the harm. Auto‑grading uses rubric for insight and mitigation ideas.
- **CSTA:** E4‑CAS‑ET‑02 (Investigate intended and unintended consequences related to emerging technologies), ALG‑IM (Impacts of Algorithms).

### T36.G4.02 – Is this data or algorithm fair to everyone?

- **Short name:** Identify bias in data or a decision rule
- **Description:** Students examine simple datasets or rules and spot patterns that might be unfair or biased. For example, a dataset of "successful coders" that includes mostly boys, or an algorithm that approves loans at different rates for different groups. They learn to question: "Who benefits? Who might be harmed?"
- **Challenge format:** Concept, data analysis and discussion. Show a dataset (e.g., toy preferences by gender, word associations, image classification samples) or a simple rule ("tall people are athletes"). Students identify who might be left out or stereotyped, and how it could cause harm. Auto‑grading uses rubric for bias identification and empathy reasoning.
- **CSTA:** DAA‑IM (Impacts of Data Science: bias and fairness), CAS‑ET (ethics).

### T36.G4.03 – Create a presentation about your project

- **Short name:** Make slides or a poster to show your work
- **Description:** Students create a presentation (using CreatiCode presentation tools, slides, or poster format) that explains their project: what it does, how they built it, what was hard, and what they learned. They practice organizing information for an audience.
- **Challenge format:** Coding and communication, project presentation. Students use CreatiCode to create a short slideshow (3–5 slides) or verbal presentation script that covers: Project name and goal, What I built, How it works, What I learned. Auto‑grading checks for completeness using a rubric and keyword matching on recorded presentation.
- **CSTA:** PRO‑PM (Communicate about your project for different audiences).

### T36.G4.04 – Ethics: Is my project respectful?

- **Short name:** Check if your project treats people fairly
- **Description:** Students reflect on a project they built or are planning: Does it include characters from different backgrounds? Does it stereotype anyone? Could it harm someone? They develop an internal ethical checklist when making creative computational work.
- **Challenge format:** Concept and reflection, design review. Before or after completing a CreatiCode project (game, story, simulation), students answer: "Does my project show people from different backgrounds? Does it use fair, respectful language? Could anyone be hurt by it? What would I change?" Auto‑grading uses rubric for thoughtfulness and action taken.
- **CSTA:** CAS‑ET (Create computing technologies that are inclusive), ALG‑IM (fairness and bias awareness).

## Grade 5

Grade 5 emphasizes analyzing the limits of current technologies, understanding how multiple professions collaborate using computing, and developing skills in professional communication and inclusive design.

### T36.G5.01 – What are the limits of this technology?

- **Short name:** Explain what a technology cannot do
- **Description:** Students analyze a real technology and articulate its limitations: what it can't do well, who it doesn't help, what problems it doesn't solve. They develop critical, nuanced thinking about technology as a tool with boundaries, not a universal solution.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, analysis and presentation. Choose a technology (social media, GPS, recommendation algorithms, AI image generator, educational app). Students investigate and present: "This technology is good at ___. It is not good at ___. It could harm ___ by ___." Auto‑grading uses rubric for accuracy, insight, and fairness.
- **CSTA:** E5‑CAS‑ET‑02 (Analyze the limitations of existing technologies and how emerging technologies change the way people work, behave, and communicate).

### T36.G5.02 – People from different jobs work together

- **Short name:** Show how different experts collaborate with computing
- **Description:** Students explore real examples where professionals from different fields (engineer, artist, biologist, teacher, doctor) work together using computing to solve a problem or create something. They see that computing bridges many disciplines and career paths.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, research and presentation. Choose a real-world project (game development, medical app, climate model, art installation). Research: What jobs are involved? What does each person do? How does computing help them work together? Present findings. Auto‑grading uses rubric for completeness, accuracy, and insight into collaboration.
- **CSTA:** E5‑CAS‑CE‑03 (Investigate how professionals collaborate with computing technologies to solve problems creatively, accurately, and efficiently), ALG‑IM (Impacts of Algorithms in professional contexts).

### T36.G5.03 – Explain your project design choices

- **Short name:** Document why you made certain choices
- **Description:** Students write or present an explanation of the major design decisions in a completed CreatiCode project: why they chose certain characters, colors, rules, or features; how they thought about making it fair, fun, or accessible; what they would change if they had more time.
- **Challenge format:** Coding and communication, design document. After completing a substantial project, students write a 1–2 page design document (or video) covering: Main idea, Why I chose [this character/setting/rule], How I made sure it's [fun/fair/clear], If I had more time, I would _____. Auto‑grading uses rubric for reflection depth and clarity.
- **CSTA:** PRO‑PM (Document program purpose, design, and development), CAS‑ET (intentional ethical design).

### T36.G5.04 – Respectful feedback and revision

- **Short name:** Give and receive helpful feedback
- **Description:** Students practice giving constructive criticism ("I liked ___ because ___. A suggestion: ___") and receiving feedback without defensiveness. They revise a project based on peer feedback, learning that iteration improves quality and that respect matters in critique.
- **Challenge format:** Coding and collaboration, peer review and revision. Students exchange projects in pairs; each student gives written or verbal feedback using a template: "One thing that worked well: ___. One thing I found confusing: ___. A suggestion: ___." The original creator uses feedback to make at least one revision. Auto‑grading checks for respectful tone and meaningful revision.
- **CSTA:** PRO‑PM (Collaborate and communicate; iterate based on feedback), Inclusive Collaboration pillar (communicate respectfully).

## Grade 6

In middle school, students deepen their understanding of how ethics, bias, and fairness apply to data and algorithms in real systems; they also explore career pathways in computing and strengthen professional communication skills.

### T36.G6.01 – Recognize bias in data and algorithms

- **Short name:** Spot and analyze bias in real systems
- **Description:** Students examine real or realistic examples of bias in data-driven systems: hiring algorithms that discriminate, facial recognition that works better for some skin tones, recommendation systems that exclude certain groups. They analyze why bias happens and propose improvements.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, case study analysis. Present a real case (e.g., Amazon's hiring algorithm, Google Images bias, loan approval algorithms) or a simplified version. Students identify: What bias exists? Who is harmed? Why did it happen? How could it be fixed? Auto‑grading uses rubric for analysis depth and fairness reasoning.
- **CSTA:** DAA‑IM (Impacts of Data Science: bias and fairness), CAS‑ET (ethical decision-making).

### T36.G6.02 – How computational thinking helps in different careers

- **Short name:** Explain how professionals use CS to solve problems
- **Description:** Students research professionals in various fields (medicine, environmental science, entertainment, business, sports) and explain how those professionals use computational thinking (breaking problems into steps, finding patterns, testing solutions) to do their work better.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, research and presentation. Choose a career or industry; research and present: "In [field], professionals use [computational thinking strategy] to [solve problem]. An example: ___." Create a 2–3 minute presentation or poster. Auto‑grading uses rubric for accuracy, clarity, and examples.
- **CSTA:** MS‑CAS‑CE‑08 (Analyze how professionals in different careers use computational thinking to solve real-world problems).

### T36.G6.03 – Ethics in a career: dilemmas and decisions

- **Short name:** Discuss ethical challenges in computing jobs
- **Description:** Students explore scenarios where computing professionals face ethical dilemmas: Should we collect this data? Should this AI be deployed? Is this privacy violation worth the convenience? They practice reasoning through competing values and considering impacts on diverse groups.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, scenario discussion and reasoning. Present a dilemma (e.g., a social media company wants to use user photos in ads without asking, a school considers using facial recognition, a game developer must choose between more engaging but addictive mechanics). Students identify stakeholders, competing values, and a justified decision. Auto‑grading uses rubric for reasoning quality and consideration of impacts.
- **CSTA:** MS‑CAS‑CE‑10 (Investigate how professionals in computing careers address ethical dilemmas), CAS‑ET (ethics and responsibility).

### T36.G6.04 – Inclusive design: consider diverse users

- **Short name:** Design for people with different abilities and needs
- **Description:** Students apply inclusive design principles to a project: considering users with different physical abilities, language backgrounds, learning styles, and cultural contexts. They understand that thoughtful design makes technology better for everyone.
- **Challenge format:** Coding and design, project improvement. Students take an existing CreatiCode project (or create a new one) and implement accessibility features: text descriptions (alt text) for images, color options, sound and visual feedback options, simple language, diverse characters. Auto‑grading checks for presence and effectiveness of accessibility features using code inspection and user testing guidance.
- **CSTA:** CAS‑ET (Create inclusive and accessible technologies), ALG‑HD (Human-Centered Design).

## Grade 7

Grade 7 emphasizes understanding how technology drives innovation across industries, examining ethical challenges in emerging tech, and developing professional communication and leadership in collaborative projects.

### T36.G7.01 – How tech transforms industries and jobs

- **Short name:** Analyze how technology changes careers and industries
- **Description:** Students examine a specific industry (agriculture, manufacturing, healthcare, entertainment) and trace how computing and automation have changed the jobs available, the skills needed, and the opportunities for workers. They consider both positive changes (new careers) and challenges (job displacement, need for retraining).
- **Challenge format:** Concept, research and analysis. Choose an industry; research and present: "Computing has changed [industry] by: ___. New jobs created: ___. Jobs displaced: ___. Skills workers now need: ___. Overall impact: ___." Create a report or presentation. Auto‑grading uses rubric for research depth, balanced analysis, and clarity.
- **CSTA:** MS‑CAS‑CE‑09 (Examine how changes in technology can create new jobs or change how people work).

### T36.G7.02 – Ethical evaluation of emerging technologies

- **Short name:** Evaluate a new technology for ethics and fairness
- **Description:** Students select an emerging technology (AI, VR, IoT, blockchain, autonomous vehicles, facial recognition) and conduct a thorough ethical evaluation: What is it designed to do? Who benefits? Who might be harmed? Is it fair? Is it accessible? What policies should govern it?
- **Challenge format:** Concept, research and argumentation. Students research an emerging tech and prepare a written or oral argument addressing: Purpose and benefits, Risks and ethical concerns, Impacts on different groups, Proposed guidelines or regulations. Auto‑grading uses rubric for ethical reasoning, evidence, and consideration of multiple perspectives.
- **CSTA:** MS‑CAS‑ET‑07 (Explain how emerging technologies create new solutions that depend on expertise from multiple fields), CAS‑ET (ethical evaluation and responsibility).

### T36.G7.03 – Lead a team project with clear communication

- **Short name:** Manage a collaborative project from start to finish
- **Description:** Students take on a leadership role in a multi-person, multi-week CreatiCode project. They practice planning (breaking tasks into steps, assigning roles), communicating clearly (holding meetings, writing updates), managing time and conflicts, and presenting results professionally.
- **Challenge format:** Coding and project management, full project cycle. Students form teams of 3–4, plan a mid-complexity project (game, simulation, interactive story), assign roles (project lead, lead coder, designer, tester), hold brief weekly check-ins, and present results. Auto‑grading uses rubric for planning quality, communication clarity, role management, and final deliverable.
- **CSTA:** PRO‑PM (Actively manage projects and teams), Inclusive Collaboration pillar (communicate and collaborate).

### T36.G7.04 – Reflect on fairness in your team and project

- **Short name:** Assess your team's inclusivity and your project's fairness
- **Description:** Students reflect on two aspects: (1) Did our team work fairly—did everyone contribute, were decisions made inclusively, was credit given? (2) Is our project inclusive and fair—does it include diverse characters, avoid stereotypes, work well for people with different abilities?
- **Challenge format:** Concept and reflection, self-assessment and peer feedback. Students complete a reflection worksheet: Team fairness (Did everyone have a say? Was work distributed fairly? How did we handle disagreement?). Project fairness (Who is represented in our project? Could anyone be harmed or offended? What changes would improve it?). Peers provide feedback. Auto‑grading uses rubric for honesty, insight, and action.
- **CSTA:** CAS‑ET (reflect on ethics and impacts), Inclusive Collaboration pillar (respect and inclusivity), ALG‑IM (fairness and bias).

## Grade 8

Grade 8 prepares students for high school by asking them to synthesize their understanding of ethics, computing careers, and responsible collaboration; to engage in real-world design challenges with ethical considerations; and to develop mature professional communication.

### T36.G8.01 – Analyze societal impacts of computing technologies

- **Short name:** Examine how a technology impacts society and inequality
- **Description:** Students conduct a research project analyzing how a widely used technology (social media, smartphones, AI assistants, cloud services, surveillance systems, e-commerce) impacts different groups of people, considering access, equity, environmental effects, cultural impacts, and power dynamics. They present nuanced, evidence-based arguments.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, research and presentation. Students choose a technology and investigate: How is it used? Who has access and who doesn't? Who profits and who bears costs? What are positive and negative societal effects? Are there disparities in impact across groups? Create a research report or presentation with evidence. Auto‑grading uses rubric for research quality, analysis sophistication, evidence use, and consideration of equity.
- **CSTA:** MS‑CAS‑ET‑06 (Describe ways that emerging technologies have influenced the development of new solutions or transformed existing processes across society and the environment), CAS‑ET (societal and environmental impacts).

### T36.G8.02 – Explore a computing career pathway

- **Short name:** Investigate a specific computing career and its requirements
- **Description:** Students deep-dive into a computing-related career of interest (game developer, data scientist, cybersecurity specialist, AI researcher, software engineer, digital artist, tech policy analyst, etc.), researching education requirements, skills needed, salary range, daily tasks, and how computing interacts with their values and interests. They assess fit with their own aspirations.
- **Challenge format:** Concept, research and presentation. Students interview a professional (if possible) or research online, and present: What does this job involve daily? What skills and education are needed? Why might someone choose this career? How does it align with my interests? Create a "career profile" poster, video, or written report. Auto‑grading uses rubric for research depth, clarity, and personal reflection.
- **CSTA:** MS‑CAS‑CE‑08, HS‑CAS‑CE‑10 (Career exploration and alignment with interests).

### T36.G8.03 – Design a solution with ethics at the center

- **Short name:** Create a project that solves a problem fairly
- **Description:** Students identify a real problem or need (accessibility barrier, environmental challenge, educational gap, social issue) and design a CreatiCode project or prototype that attempts to solve it while explicitly considering: Who is this for? What biases might we introduce? How do we include diverse perspectives in the design? How will we know if it actually helps?
- **Challenge format:** Coding and design, open-ended challenge with ethical framework. Students brainstorm problems, propose a computational solution, document their design thinking (who benefits, potential harms, mitigation), build a prototype in CreatiCode, and test with users or peers. Auto‑grading uses rubric for problem relevance, solution feasibility, ethical reasoning, and evidence of user-centered iteration.
- **CSTA:** CAS‑ET (Create inclusive and accessible computing; consider ethical impacts), ALG‑HD (Human-Centered Design), PRO‑PM (Design and document projects).

### T36.G8.04 – Communicate professionally about your work

- **Short name:** Present your project professionally to diverse audiences
- **Description:** Students develop a polished presentation or documentation of a substantial project suitable for multiple audiences (classmates, teachers, parents, competition judges, potential employers). They practice adapting their communication to audience, using technical vocabulary appropriately, and conveying both technical details and broader impact.
- **Challenge format:** Coding and communication, professional presentation. Students create: (1) A technical document describing the project (goals, design, code approach, challenges). (2) A presentation (slides, video, or live talk) for a general audience. (3) A reflection on lessons learned and impact. Auto‑grading uses rubric for clarity, completeness, audience awareness, and professionalism.
- **CSTA:** PRO‑PM (Communicate effectively; document and present projects), Inclusive Collaboration pillar (communicate for different audiences).

## Summary of Skill Progression

**K–2 (Foundational):**
Students develop awareness that technology is used everywhere, that it has both benefits and harms, and that working together kindly and fairly is important. They begin to practice basic communication and turn-taking.

**3–5 (Developing):**
Students deepen reflection on technology choices, fairness, and impacts. They explore the role of computing in different careers, document and present their work, and practice giving and receiving feedback. Ethical thinking begins to be applied to their own projects.

**6–8 (Proficient):**
Students analyze bias and ethics in real systems, research professional computing roles and career pathways, and lead collaborative projects with inclusive design principles. They engage with emerging technologies critically and practice professional communication with multiple audiences. By Grade 8, students are prepared for high school-level work in ethics, careers, and responsible computing.
